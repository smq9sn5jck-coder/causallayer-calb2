## What changed in this release

The benchmark now carries **two ground-truth columns** instead of one:

1. **`liability_allocation`** — what the court actually ruled (the legal-responsibility answer key, present since v0.1).
2. **`causal_allocation`** — what *technically went wrong* in the system, derived deterministically from documented case fields (the causation answer key, **new in v0.3**).

This separation reflects a finding from the v0.1 publication: legal-responsibility allocation and causal attribution answer different questions, and grading both engine outputs against a single legal column unfairly penalised the causal axis.

The causal answer key is generated by the deterministic enricher described in `dataset/CAUSAL_GROUND_TRUTH_RULES.md`. Every value carries the rule that produced it, so the enrichment is fully reproducible from the same case data.

## Engine v0.7 doctrine refinements

Two new deterministic doctrines were added to the legal-responsibility scorer:

* **Strict Product Liability / Vertically-Integrated Producer.** Triggers when the deployer is also the system's producer (deployer built or substantially configured the AI). Liability collapses onto the deployer because under strict-product-liability the producer is whoever places the product into commerce. (e.g. iTutorGroup self-built screener, Cruise AV, Cleo AI, Air Canada chatbot.)
* **Negligent Deployment / Constructive Knowledge.** Triggers when the deployer had constructive notice of system limitations (`training_data_known_biases=true` and/or `known_failure_modes_disclosed=true` and/or `incident_was_predictable=true`) and deployed without adequate human-in-the-loop. The doctrine collapses Comparative Negligence onto the deployer because the deployer's negligence is the proximate legal cause regardless of the AI's contribution. (e.g. Detroit FRT wrongful arrest, Rite Aid FRT.)

Both doctrines are deterministic rules; the engine remains byte-identical reproducible across runs.

## Brutally honest interpretation

* The causal-axis lift (10.7% → 66.0%) is **mostly attributable to fairer grading**, not engine improvement. v0.6 was being graded against a column that does not measure causation. Once the right column exists, the engine looks much more competent on causation. Honest readers should consider this a benchmarking-discipline lift more than an engine-quality lift.
* The legal axis dropped 1.9 pp because v0.7 is **more conservative** about Comparative Negligence — it now collapses to deployer-only when the deployer was on notice. On a few cases the v0.6 50/50 was incidentally closer to the court's ruling than v0.7's deterministic 100/0. Across the board, allocations are more precise (L1 -12.7%).
* Per-doctrine breakdown remains uneven; small-sample doctrines (Equitable Apportionment n=4, Strict Product Liability n=7) carry low confidence.
* FRCP Rule 11 cases (n=75) continue to dominate the corpus. CALB-2 v0.4 should diversify.

## Roadmap for v0.4

* Diversify away from FRCP Rule 11 — target ≥40% of cases outside the legal-services hallucination cluster.
* Expand Strict Product Liability and Comparative Negligence sample sizes.
* Add EU AI Act cases as they begin to be adjudicated post-August 2026.
* Add a `secondary_ground_truth_columns` field for cases where the appellate court substantially modified the trial court allocation.
